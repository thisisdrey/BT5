# Burn Instead of Moving Funds to `RewardContract`

## Summary
The client has acknowledged an issue with the `RewardPool.sol` contract. The `burn()` and `move()` functions in the contract have almost identical logic, but the only difference is where the transferred amount goes. This lack of distinction between the two functions could lead to misuse and confusion. The recommendation is to include the function selector in the signature scheme to ensure that signatures are specific to each function, preventing unintended burning or moving of funds. This will enhance the security and correctness of fund movements within the contract.

## Details
**Update**
The client acknowledged the issue and provided the following explanation:

> Burn is used for the tokens in the reward pool during the upgrade of Netmind's new token model. This upgrade has been completed and will no longer be used.

**File(s) affected:**`RewardPool.sol`

**Description:** The `burn()` and `move()` functions in the contract exhibit nearly identical logic, with the primary distinction being the use of separate nonces (`BurnNonce` and `MoveNonce`). Both functions perform similar checks on the nonce, amount, expiry, and signatures. The only substantial difference is the destination of the transferred amount: the `move()` function transfers the amount to `RewardContract`, whereas the `burn()` function transfers the amount to the zero address. This lack of distinction between the two functions could lead to potential misuse and confusion, especially if the nonces become aligned and the signatures have not expired. The reliance on nonce alignment as the sole protection mechanism is fragile and could be compromised, leading to unintended burning of funds that were meant to be moved to the reward pool, or vice versa.

**Recommendation:** Include the function selector in the signature scheme to ensure that signatures are specific to each function. This means the data signed should explicitly indicate whether the operation is a `burn` or a `move`, thereby preventing one function from being called with a signature intended for the other. This additional validation step ensures that the correct function is being authorized by the signatures, thus enhancing the security and correctness of fund movements within the contract.
