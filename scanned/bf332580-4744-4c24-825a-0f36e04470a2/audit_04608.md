# Blind auction bid amount can 't resist brute-force attack 

## Summary
The bug report discusses an issue with the blind bidding process in an auction contract. Bidders are required to make a blind bid and then reveal the bid amount. However, the bid amount can be easily guessed through a brute-force attack, making it vulnerable to manipulation. To address this issue, the report recommends introducing a random nonce to increase security. The bug has been fixed in a recent update, which also allows for multiple blind bids from a single account and adds a listing ID to the hashing process for further protection against manipulation.

## Details
## Auction Contract Vulnerability Analysis

## Context 
- **File:** Auction.sol
- **Line:** 254

## Description
When bidders enter the blind auction, they make a blind bid and then reveal the bid amount. When making a blind bid, the protocol saves a hash for the bidder. When revealing the bid amount, it requires that the `bidAmount` provided by the bidder meets the following condition:

```
keccak256(encodeUserBid(bidAmount_, from, tokenId, nft)) == savedHash
```

The parameters `from`, `tokenId`, and `nft` are all public; only `bidAmount` will be made public when the bidder reveals it. However, considering that `bidAmount` is non-random and related to the market value of the NFT, it is not resistant to brute-force attacks.

For example, for an NFT with a market price of 1000 USDC, the bidders' `bidAmount` may be in the range of `[0, 1000]` USDC. Given that USDC is 6 decimals, 1e10 calculations will be enough to brute-force all the `bidAmount`. If the user's bid may be in the smallest unit of `0.01` USDC, the required calculations will be even fewer.

## Recommendation
It is recommended to introduce a user-supplied random nonce to increase the hashing strength.

## KIM Exchange
- **Fix Implemented:** Fixed in PR 8.

By utilizing `mapping(uint256 listingId => bytes32[] bids) public blindBidsForListing;`, we allow the following possibilities:
1. Sending multiple blind bids from a single account. For example, blind bids for 999, 1000, and 1001, 1002, while only revealing one of them later.
2. Sending a blind bid from a different account and then revealing a blind bid later with the correct `from` account by specifying the correct blind bid index.

This change makes guessing a blind bid significantly more difficult.

## Cantina Managed
The fix makes it more challenging to brute-force blind bids, especially since the bidder becomes uncertain. As an additional measure, `listingID` was added to the hashing process to protect against pattern matching of revealed hashes for future blind auctions.
