# Untrusted Epochs Can Be Exploited by One Malicious Validator, Resulting in Finalization of Incorrect Blocks

## Summary
The client has marked the bug as "Fixed" and provided an explanation for the issue. The bug affects the `eth_headers.rs` file and can potentially cause incorrect blocks to be finalized, which could impact other components of the project. The exploit scenario involves a malicious validator, Alice, introducing fake headers and signatures to pass the verification process and finalize incorrect blocks. The recommendation is to ensure that the first block of an epoch is always finalized before the new checkpoint and to consider implementing different policies for different chains.

## Details
**Update**
Marked as "Fixed" by the client. Addressed in: `3bf2a1c2a1e9bbbd00626a012d8353c0474ce291`. The client provided the following explanation:

> When verifying BLS aggregated attestations from validators in untrusted epochs, we have modified the validation to ensure that at least 1/3 of the previous valid validators' votes are included in the attestation. This verifies that at least one honest validator from the previous valid validators is participating in the block finalization.

**File(s) affected:**`eth_headers.rs`

**Description:** Suppose we want to finalize a target block `x` with reference to a trusted block `w`.

In order to finalize `x`, it is required to provide a consecutive sequence of headers starting at `x` and ending in two adjacent headers (call them the "finalizing blocks" `y` and `z`) that contain BLS signatures from their corresponding active validator set.

At the start of every epoch, a new validator set is elected. This validator set becomes active once an epoch-specific checkpoint is reached: before the checkpoint, headers must be signed by validators from the trusted epoch; starting from the checkpoint, headers are required to be signed by the new validator set; before the checkpoint, BLS signatures used to finalize blocks must come from validators of the trusted epoch; _after_ the checkpoint, such BLS signatures must come from the new validator set. This process updates with every subsequent validator set election.

Ordinarily, finalizing incorrect blocks is difficult because 14 out of 21 validators of an active validator set would need to sign BLS signatures for two consecutive blocks in order to finalize a block that comes prior. The light client also ensures that at least 8 validators in an untrusted epoch are from the previous trusted epoch. However, it appears to be possible for one malicious validator, Alice, to introduce 13 malicious validators into an untrusted validator set along with herself in the following scenarios. Along with (or inside) an incorrect target block, Alice transmits a fake new validator set and a sequence of fake headers that she signs herself in order to pass the `verify_seal()` check, up to and including the checkpoint. She will also submit finalizing headers post-checkpoint with BLS signatures coming from the malicious validator set, meeting quorum. See the "Exploit Scenario" section below for an example-based explanation of the issue.

In the worst case, the finalization of incorrect blocks may impact components of the LCP project that rely on the light client for correct data: for example, if a bridge were to be fed incorrect data regarding the state of the `IBCStore`'s storage root, it could be assumed that messages were broadcast when they in fact weren't, resulting in, for example, the issuing of funds on a destination chain without the appropriate conditions.

**Exploit Scenario:**

 Consider the following two cases:

1.   `x` and `w` are in the same epoch. In this case, `x`'s epoch is trusted and the following epoch is untrusted.
2.   `x` and `w` are not in the same epoch. In this case, it is enforced that `x` is the first block of the epoch after the epoch of `w`. Moreover, the epoch of `x` is untrusted.

Take the first case, and suppose `x` is the block before the start of a new epoch. Alice has signed this block and wishes to finalize it in the light client. Therefore, she constructs a special sequence of blocks: the new epoch block will contain a list of validators that consists of Alice's validator and 13 puppet validator accounts. Each of the blocks before and including the checkpoint will be signed by Alice and the last two blocks will come after the checkpoint, containing BLS signatures that originate from the 14 malicious validator accounts. This can cause `x`, an incorrect block, to be finalized.

The second case is similar. In this case however, Alice and the 13 puppet validators are included in the target block, and the BLS-signature-containing headers come after the checkpoint of the target header's epoch.

**Recommendation:** In general, validators from an untrusted epoch should not have their BLS signatures trusted when proving finality. As such, it would be best to ensure that the first block of an epoch is always finalized before the new checkpoint. Let us, however, consider a situation in which BLS signatures from a trusted epoch are simply not available before the checkpoint of an untrusted epoch. In such a case, a greater level of assurance can be obtained by requiring that 14 of the validators in the new epoch must come from the trusted epoch. Here, 7 validators from the trusted epoch would have to be malicious in order to finalize incorrect blocks. Finally, consider implementing different policies for different chains: for example, on testnet, where stakes are lower and vote data is not as available, it might be necessary to rely on validators from untrusted epochs; whereas on mainnet, relying on validators of untrusted epochs is much more risky and less important of a consideration.
