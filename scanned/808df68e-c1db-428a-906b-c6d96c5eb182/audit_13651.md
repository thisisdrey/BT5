# Price Oracle Functionality May Not Align with Protocol's Needs

## Summary
This bug report discusses an issue with the implementation of an oracle, which is used to determine the price of NFTs. The client is planning to use a dynamic TWAP oracle, which calculates the price based on the mid-price of bids and asks. However, this may cause problems within the protocol and could lead to manipulation. The report also mentions potential issues with other oracles, such as not updating the price data frequently enough or reporting stale data. The recommendation is to thoroughly test the oracle and consider potential attack vectors before launching on the mainnet.

## Details
**Update**
The client acknowledged the issue, explaining that they will be using an oracle that uses the mid-price of bids and asks with a dynamic TWAP. We note that the implementation of the oracle is out of scope for this audit. As the team selects an oracle, we encourage rigorous testing of the mentioned attack vectors before the mainnet launch.

The client’s full explanation is as follows:

"We are working to improve the current construct of the TWAP oracle. We are contemplating using an oracle that uses the mid-price of bids (best collection bid) and asks (lowest listed floor price) as the base data, with a dynamic TWAP period. With the popularity of Blur - bids / asks spread has been very tight and now a credible source of value gauge for blue chip collections. Using the mid-price of bids and asks would be similar to how one determines price by looking at an order book trading system. To further reduce the risk of manipulation, a TWAP of the mid-price will be used, and the period of TWAP will be determined by the market activity / volatility. There are 3 cases of market activity / volatility:

1.   Business as usual: number of transactions happening is in line with normal market activity, underlying market price or value remains relatively stable. In this case, the baseline TWAP time period (i.e. 3 hours) is preferred as we don’t want the oracle price to be too sensitive since the underlying market price is not changing too much.

2.   Low market volatility: number of transactions happening is much lower than usual and the underlying market price is very stable. In this case, a longer TWAP time period is preferred as the underlying market price isn’t moving much and with market being inactive, it is relatively easier to manipulate compared to the baseline market activity level.

3.   High market volatility, the number of transactions happening is much higher than usual, and the underlying market price is very volatile. In this case, a shorter TWAP time period is preferred as we want the oracle price to track the underlying market movement more quickly to ensure that liquidations are done in a timely manner and also that funding rate calculation and repeg are done properly. In this case, manipulation risk is also not a big concern since the underlying market activity level is high, so it is hard to manipulate the market even though TWAP time period is shorter.

The way we achieve such a dynamic TWAP period construct is to establish a normal market activity (e.g. median number of transactions done in 3 hours for the past 30 days) and compare it with the actual number of transactions that happened in the last 3 hours. If the actual market activity level > median market activity level, we DECREASE the TWAP period from the baseline TWAP to make the oracle more sensitive. If the actual market activity level < median market activity level, we INCREASE the TWAP from the baseline TWAP to make the oracle less sensitive."

**File(s) affected:**`ChainlinkPriceFeed.sol`

**Description:** NFT floor price oracles generally claim to provide a robust/attack-resistant approach to computing the price, using techniques such as aggregating data from multiple NFT markets, and statistical approaches to eliminate outliers. However, the implementation details of these oracles are not fully apparent, and a variety of issues could occur depending on their approach. For example:

1.   If the oracle uses a TWAP to compute the price floor, this may be problematic within Tribe3 as this data is fed into another TWAP (effectively we'd have a "TWAP of TWAPs"). This may cause the oracle price trajectory to be "slowed" compared to the actual price.
2.   Some oracles may choose to not update the price data if it remains within some threshold (e.g., if the computed price does not move by more than 2%, the oracle will not update). While this discrepancy may be fine for some applications, a 1-2% shift could have a significant impact on Tribe3 users.
3.   If a flash crash occurs on an NFT collection, a 3h oracle update period may be too slow.
4.   Some Chainlink oracles such as for the [BAYC floor price](https://etherscan.io/address/0x352f2Bc3039429fC2fe62004a1575aE74001CfcE#readContract) appear to currently be reporting stale data, returning `68.6 ETH` for a full day.
5.   If an oracle doesn't properly protect against wash trading, an attacker can sell an NFT to themselves for lower and lower prices while opening a short position on the Tribe3 vAMM. This would force continuous payout, block trade whenever the price fluctuates by more than 2%, and result in the liquidation of many users.

**Recommendation:** Ensure that the used price feeds are aligned with the protocol's implementation. Consider the relevant attack vectors based on the known details of the oracle's implementation.
